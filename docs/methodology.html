Narnia Annotation Methodology
Research Methodology

From raw text to annotated corpus

Traditional NLP pipelines require task-specific engineering — custom NER models, hand-crafted rules, separate entity recognizers — for each new annotation schema. This project demonstrates that a general-purpose LLM can handle a novel, multi-part annotation task out of the box, with no fine-tuning and no labeled training data.

The claim: LLMs are more general than pre-defined NLP pipelines.

Raw data — the source text

The full text of The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe was split into 17 chapter files, one sentence per line. Each sentence was assigned a sequential sentence_id within its chapter. No pre-annotation, no preprocessing beyond sentence splitting.

Task definition — two sub-tasks in one prompt

The annotation task combines two traditionally separate NLP problems into a single pass: entity recognition (which canonical character does this surface mention refer to?) and agency-based NER (what role does the character play in this sentence?).

Sub-task 1 — Entity recognition

Map surface forms to canonical character identities. The model must learn that "Faun", "the Faun", and "Tumnus" all refer to the same character.

"Faun", "the Faun", "Tumnus"→Mr. Tumnus
"the White Witch", "the Queen", "Her Imperial Majesty"→Jadis
"Ed", "Edmund"→Edmund Pevensie
"the Lion", "Aslan"→Aslan
"the Professor", "Professor Kirke"→Professor Digory Kirke

Sub-task 2 — Agency classification

Classify each mention by the character's narrative role in that sentence, using 6 labels with nuanced distinctions.

ACTIVE_SPEAKER Character is speaking
ACTIVE_PERFORMER Physical action
ACTIVE_THOUGHT Internal state or feeling
ADDRESSED Spoken to directly
MENTIONED_ONLY Third-person reference
MISCELLANEOUS Species/group label
The key challenge: These distinctions are subtle. "Lucy was tired" → ACTIVE_THOUGHT, not ACTIVE_PERFORMER. "Oh, Mr. Tumnus!" → ADDRESSED, not MENTIONED_ONLY. Pre-built NER systems have no label for these distinctions — they would need to be engineered from scratch.

Prompt design — combining both tasks in one pass

The final prompt runs entity recognition and agency classification together, returning structured CSV directly. No intermediate steps, no post-processing pipeline.

Zero-shot prompt (chapter-level)
You are annotating a chapter from The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe for character agency.

For each sentence, identify all character mentions and assign each one an agency label.
Return ONLY a CSV with columns: sentence_id,sentence,entities
where entities is a JSON list of {"text": "...", "canonical": "...", "label": "..."} objects.
- "text" is the surface form of the mention as it appears in the sentence.
- "canonical" is the character's consistent canonical name (resolve aliases).
- "label" is the agency label for that sentence.
No explanation. Do not skip sentences. Number sentences starting from 1.

Valid labels: ACTIVE_SPEAKER, ACTIVE_PERFORMER, ACTIVE_THOUGHT, ADDRESSED, MENTIONED_ONLY, MISCELLANEOUS

Key distinctions:
- A character called by name in speech ("Oh, Mr. Tumnus!") is ADDRESSED, not MENTIONED_ONLY.
- A character's feeling or internal state ("Lucy was tired") is ACTIVE_THOUGHT, not ACTIVE_PERFORMER.
- "Son of Adam" / "Daughter of Eve" used as direct address → ADDRESSED; as species label → MISCELLANEOUS.
- If a sentence has no character mentions, return [].

Canonical name mappings (resolve these automatically):
- Faun / the Faun / Tumnus → Mr. Tumnus
- Ed → Edmund Pevensie
- the White Witch / the Queen / Jadis / Her Imperial Majesty → Jadis
- the Lion / Aslan → Aslan
...
The output format is structured CSV — parseable, comparable, and ready for analysis without any model-specific post-processing. The model handles tokenization, span detection, alias resolution, and label classification in a single inference call.

Analysis — what the annotations reveal

Aggregating across all chapters surfaces patterns that are hard to see from reading alone. Three findings stand out.

Finding 1

The Aslan/Jadis paradox

The two most powerful characters in the story have the lowest active agency ratios — Aslan at 38.8% and Jadis at 39.4%. Both are more often talked about than acting within individual sentences. The model captured this structural feature of Lewis's storytelling without being told to look for it.

Finding 2

Chapter density tracks narrative arc

Character mention density (mentions per sentence) peaks at chapters 8 and 15 — the Beavers' escape and the final battle. Chapter 1 is the sparsest (0.72 mentions/sentence), reflecting its role as scene-setting with few character interactions. The model's output is legible as a structural signal of narrative intensity.

Finding 3

Alias resolution holds across 17 chapters

Edmund and Lucy each receive ~411 mentions despite being referred to by dozens of surface forms ("Ed", "Edmund", "the boy", "Son of Adam", "her brother", etc.) across thousands of sentences. The tight mention counts reflect consistent canonical name resolution — the model never drifts across long contexts.

The result: A complete, structured, queryable annotation of a 17-chapter novel — produced by prompting a general-purpose LLM with a task description and 9 examples. No fine-tuning. No custom pipeline. No task-specific engineering.
